The customer in one sentence
Our ideal customer is —

A funded, software-dependent company in a high-stakes or regulated domain — fintech and lending first — that built its product fast, usually with AI, and now has to make it production-grade and scale it — but whose own team can't get it there to the bar the business requires.

It's an AND, not an OR. All three filters below have to be true. That's what separates a real prospect from "anyone with a budget."

The three filters · all must be true

What Makes a Fit

Filter 1 · Domain

Fintech & Lending First

Fintech, lending, mortgage, private credit, banking-adjacent. It's where we have real reps (LoanCAD) and where bad software is most dangerous and most expensive — so the buyer won't shop on hourly rate. Operations-heavy / regulated B2B (insurance, healthcare-adjacent, edtech) is the expansion ring.

Filter 2 · Stage & budget

Funded, Real Money

Funded (seed–Series B) or profitably investing in software. Roughly 10–150 people with a small or stretched engineering team. Real budget, real urgency, and a revenue / risk / compliance reason it has to be right.

Filter 3 · Trigger

Something Just Broke Loose

At least one trigger is firing right now (see below). Capability isn't enough — there has to be live pain and a reason to act this quarter, not "someday."

What to listen for

Buying Triggers

At least one of these should be true. The first is the wedge — the clearest, most common pain, and where we're strongest. Lead with it.

01

An AI-built product has to scale The wedge

A funded startup built its MVP fast — with AI, no-code, or a rushed sprint — it got traction or funding, and now it has to become production-grade: real architecture, auth, security, and the ability to scale. The buyer already knows they have this problem. This is the main event.

02

Something shipped is hitting a wall

A product already live is fragile, insecure, slow, or failing a security / compliance check. They need experienced people to stabilize it before it costs them a customer or a breach.

03

The internal team is buried

They have engineers, but they're consumed by roadmap and support, and one important initiative is stuck. They need a senior team to own it end-to-end — without adding management overhead.

04

Moving fast on AI with no senior cover

Velocity is up, but no senior architect is steering it, so quality and security quietly drift. Real need — but usually latent, and it converts after we've proven ourselves on a build, not from a cold "training" pitch. Treat it as expansion, not a wedge.

Who you're talking to

The Buyer

Technical enough to respect senior engineering, constrained enough to need outside hands. And often the best intro comes from their investor.

Technical founder CTO Head of Engineering VP Engineering / Product COO / CEO (smaller co.) Operating partner (PE/VC)

Referrer note: a fintech-focused VC's portfolio is a roomful of these companies hitting the wall at once. The Success Academy intro came through a VC founder list — that's a channel, not luck.

Qualify fast

Green Flags / Red Flags

Lean in

  • Recently funded, or profitable and already spending on software
  • Names a specific, painful, time-sensitive problem
  • Has an engineering team (even a small or stretched one)
  • Worked with agencies or contractors before
  • In or adjacent to a regulated / high-stakes domain
  • Built their product fast with AI and is now trying to scale it

Walk away

  • Keeps asking for a rate card or hourly comparison
  • No named internal owner for the project
  • Bootstrapped, idea-stage, no real budget
  • Wants pure staff-augmentation (bodies, not ownership)
  • Mature eng org that keeps everything in-house
  • Non-technical buyer who treats software as a commodity

How to source
