Three parts: process changes SA can adopt now, the framework we'd build, and where the teams stand with AI today.

Process & Org Changes

What SA adopts now

The problem

Today, a project moves through the three teams one after another — Product hands to AI Engineering, which hands to Cloud Engineering. Each team waits on the one before it, so work starts late and the crunch compounds onto whoever's last in line. When the deadline slips, it lands on Tech, even though the delay started upstream.

Product AI Engineering Cloud Engineering

The recommendation

None of this needs new tooling or a new framework — just a standing weekly meeting and a shift in discipline that lets the three teams work in parallel instead of waiting in line. That alone compresses the Product → AI Engineering → Cloud Engineering timeline. The point is to get the three teams overlapping instead of running one after another.

Product AI Engineering Cloud Engineering
01
Recurring weekly · Product + AI Engineering

Product & AI Eng Weekly Check-in

Same day every week. Both teams walk through every product currently in flight — in Product's hands or AI Engineering's — and update each other on what's new or changed since last week. Each product's delivery date is part of that update, so both teams stay aligned on timelines.

New products AI Engineering hasn't seen yet get surfaced here too. For each, AI Engineering asks high-level questions — auth, data warehouse access, integrations, scale, anything Lovable can't carry on its own. If they want to dig deeper, they schedule a Project Briefing.

02
Per project · Product + Vadim (AI Eng)

Project Briefing

Just the Product person and Vadim from AI Engineering. Product walks Vadim through what they currently understand about the project — not a finished spec, and Product isn't expected to have one yet. Things will change.

The point is to give AI Engineering enough of a picture to start researching and thinking about what the project might need. Vadim starts the Requirements Doc with whatever Product knows so far. It evolves from there.

03
Per project · Doc + dedicated Slack channel

Requirements Doc

Every project has one central Requirements Doc. It's the gold standard — the doc, not the conversation, is the source of truth.

Each project also gets a dedicated Slack channel that both teams use to discuss the work. Claude is in the channel. When something said in Slack needs to land in the Doc, anyone can @claude and the update gets made.

Email and in-office conversations are fine too, but those need to be brought back into the Doc manually.

04
After the Project Briefing · AI Eng + Infra

Engineering Head Start

After the Project Briefing, AI Engineering and Infra have a high-level read on what the project will need. The Head Start is the time and space for them to act on that read — to start working in parallel with Product instead of waiting until Product is done.

How they use that time is up to them. Common moves:

  • AI Engineering sets up a branch and starts an initial iteration of the project.
  • Infra stands up the sandbox environment and gets the CI/CD pipeline ready.

The high-level reqs could still change. But they tend to be the core pieces of the project, so the risk of throwaway work is low — and the timeline savings (the parallelization shown above) are real.

05
New role · DRI for delivery

Delivery Lead

The Delivery Lead is the DRI — the single accountable owner — for each project landing on time. This role is filled by a third-party, unbiased person — not from inside the Product team — so their judgment stays neutral. They don't own scope, engineering choices, or design. They own delivery.

Today, when a project slips, blame lands on Tech (AI Engineering and Cloud Engineering) because they're always last in line. Whatever fell behind upstream still has to be wrapped up by Tech before launch. The Delivery Lead changes that. Because they're not tied to any one team, they're incentivized to hold each team — Product, AI Engineering, Cloud Engineering — to its part of the timeline, and to flag slippage early enough to do something about it.

Open question. Reporting line is an SA-leadership call. Whoever it reports into, the role has to be measured on outcomes for all three teams — otherwise the independence breaks down.

Systems

What we'd build

The problem

Today, Product hands off work built in Lovable that Engineering can't ship as-is — and getting it to production creates a string of problems:

  • Off-standard from the start. Supabase, weak auth, no infrastructure code — none of it meets the bar for production.
  • Engineering has to scramble. They rework what they're handed into something shippable, almost always against a tight deadline.
  • That forces compromises. To hit the date, they ship shortcuts — like the stack on EC2 with Supabase baked in instead of standing it up the right way on RDS and ECS.
  • Two sources of truth. Product keeps iterating on the Lovable version while Engineering owns the reworked one, and keeping the two in sync is a constant, losing battle.

The recommendation

What we're proposing is that every Lovable project starts from an engineering-approved template. Anyone spinning up a project in Lovable begins with the right standards already in place. That takes most of the scramble off Engineering's plate — far less rework, no compromises forced by the clock, and no two-way sync to fight. Most of all, it gives the Engineering team a lot of their time and energy back.

The engineering-approved template itself is two pieces.

The monorepo

We already know what a good monorepo for a web application looks like. This one is organized into three areas:

  • Frontend. apps/web — Vite + React, Lovable's native target — plus the shared @sa/ui design system, so every project renders with SA's components and tokens.
  • Backend. Two runtimes: apps/api (Fastify) for CRUD and general backend work, and apps/python-api (FastAPI) for LLM and AI-specific work, with shared business logic in @sa/api-sdk.
  • Infrastructure. infra/ as AWS CDK — every new repo deploys to its own sandbox on the first push, with CI/CD, auth, and a shared sandbox database wired in from day one.

The Lovable wiring

The newer part — what we had to work out on this project — is the wiring that gets Lovable building inside the template instead of around it:

  • Scoped rules. A layered ruleset (a root RULES.md for every agent, plus a Lovable-only .lovable/RULES.md) tells Lovable which directories it can touch, what it can't import, and how to name endpoints.
  • The adapter pattern. The web client hits either Lovable's Supabase functions or the real Fastify backend by swapping a base URL — so engineering never has to rip Supabase out later.
  • Design-system sync. Lovable is fed SA's @sa/ui tokens and components (kept current by a CI check), so it builds with SA's design system instead of re-rolling its own.
  • Mechanical enforcement. ESLint blocks what the rules can't catch by convention — like importing the Supabase SDK into the web app.

The full build-out — folder structure, packages, and the Lovable rules — is in the appendix.

People

Where the teams stand

Saroj asked for a read on how well three of his teams are using AI in their coding. Here's what came out of those conversations — and what would get them all on the same page.

The review

Each team walked through how they're actually using AI day today — what's set up, what's working, and where the gaps are. They're all on the same line, just at different spots on it.

Salesforce · Joe Jung

Joe's the newest to this. He's set up some rules in Cursor, which is a start — but he hasn't gone past it. No context files spread through the repo (a CLAUDE.md living next to the code), no subagents to keep the context window clean, no skills. There's real upside here once the fundamentals click.

AI Engineering · Priti's team

They're all in Cursor and they've started writing skills, which is good. No subagents yet, though. They haven't set up directory-level context files either — but to be fair, their projects are small enough that those might not earn their keep yet.

Where subagents would really pay off for this team is the repetitive work: spinning up the same kind of component, endpoint, or service over and over. That's exactly what a subagent is good at.

Data Engineering · Carlos & Srikar

This team's the furthest along — Carlos especially. He's using CLAUDE.md files and context engineering has clearly started to click for him. The gap is that it's all personal. Everyone's running their own home-directory CLAUDE.md files and skills, with no shared repo behind it — so the good work one person does doesn't carry across the team. No subagents yet, and no context files living inside the repos themselves.

What's true across all three. Two things. Nobody's using subagents — the idea makes sense to people, but the why and the when haven't landed. And almost none of the context lives in the codebase: it's in personal setups and Cursor rules, not committed to the repo where the whole team — and the AI — can actually use it. Even the team that's furthest ahead keeps all of it personal.

The recommendation

The teams are at different spots, but the gaps rhyme. So the answer isn't three different plans — it's getting everyone on the same baseline first.

The recommendation is a single context engineering session for the whole org. One room, same starting point for all three teams. It would cover:

  • How the context window actually works — and what context rot is. Why stuffing everything into one window makes the model worse, not better.
  • The three tools, and when to reach for each. Context files (CLAUDE.md) for what's always true, skills for repeatable procedures, and subagents for isolating messy or repetitive work in its own clean window.
  • Directory-level context files. How to get the knowledge your developers already carry — code gotchas, how the product is supposed to behave — into the codebase itself, so the AI is sharper everywhere it works.

Then 1-1s — open to anyone who wants to sit down and work through their own codebase one-on-one. That's where the session turns into something real for each team: Carlos can push further than the room goes, Priti's team can stand up their first subagents for the repetitive stuff, and Joe can get the fundamentals solid.

This sets up the framework, too. The shared context layer in the framework — the committed CLAUDE.md files, agents, and skills — is exactly where this knowledge is meant to live. The session teaches the teams how to use it and add to it, so it keeps paying off as they build.

One template repo at the center, with four substrates attached. Each project SA spins up inherits all of them on day one — independent of which tool the person creating the project is using.

The template repo

Every new SA project starts as a fresh repo created from one shared template. The template ships everything the project needs to exist inside SA's environment — code structure, infra, CI/CD, a deployed sandbox URL, and SA's conventions baked in. The AI context layer (described below) is wired in throughout, so Claude Code and Lovable are both productive from the first commit.

Status: proven. The shape is already built and working in sa-ai-loveable-monorepo. We've verified the monorepo layout, the dual-runtime adapter pattern, and the rule-driven Lovable workflow end-to-end. The deeper detail lives in the appendices.

What's wired up out of the box:

  • Sandbox AWS environment via CDK — every new repo deploys to its own sandbox on the first push. See §2.7.
  • CI/CD via .github/workflows — build, test, deploy to sandbox automatically.
  • Auth — pulled in from @sa/auth (see §2.4). Google for student-facing apps, Okta for back-office, composable for mixed.
  • Shared sandbox database — each project gets its own schema in a hosted Postgres, not a freshly provisioned RDS.
  • Dual-runtime adapter pattern — the same web client targets either Lovable's Supabase Edge Functions or the Fastify API by swapping only the base URL. See §2.5.

Stack picks — initial recommendations, to be confirmed during the engagement:

  • apps/web — Vite + React. Lovable's native target; fast HMR; cleanest fit with the adapter pattern. Alternative to evaluate: Vercel / Next.js if Saroj prefers their deploy story.
  • apps/api — Fastify. Lean, fast, ergonomic. Alternative to evaluate: NestJS if SA wants the heavier opinionated framework with built-in DI and module conventions.
  • apps/python-api — FastAPI. The default for LLM calls, embeddings, agent orchestration.
  • Bun as the package manager — matches Lovable's harness and gives us workspace support out of the box.

Rules Lovable follows

The biggest single lever on the handoff from Product to Engineering isn't where Lovable's code lives — it's what Lovable is told to write. Every rule Lovable can reliably follow is one less thing engineering has to undo.

Status: proven in initial testing. Lovable followed the rules we set up in the prototype repo — scoping (which directories it may touch), banned imports (no @supabase/* in the web app), the adapter pattern, the design-system rules, the endpoint naming convention. Open question is sustained adherence under real product-team workload — explicitly something we'd evaluate early in the engagement.

The mechanism is layered:

  • RULES.md at the repo root — rules every agent (Lovable, Claude Code, Cursor) follows.
  • .lovable/RULES.md — Lovable-only rules covering scope, naming, the banned-import list, the env-var contract.
  • .lovable/system.md + .lovable/rules/ — the full design-system guidance (Atlas tokens, per-component, per-pattern), generated from packages/ui/lovable/ and kept in sync by a CI check.
  • ESLint enforcement for the rules that can be enforced mechanically (e.g. no-restricted-imports blocks @supabase/* in the web app).

The kinds of rules that pay off: use plain fetch instead of the Supabase SDK so engineering doesn't have to swap it later; route all data access through the per-feature client in lib/api/; name endpoints as <feature>-<action> so the same client targets either backend by swapping only the base URL; use @sa/ui primitives instead of re-rolling buttons. Each one shrinks engineering's rebuild surface area.

The full ruleset and the testing we've done lives in Appendix B — Monorepo & Lovable interop.

Shared packages

Shared packages reduce engineering's rebuild surface area across every project at every tier. A Lovable project that imports @sa/ui for components and @sa/data-warehouse-sdk for warehouse access ships less novel code that engineering has to evaluate — regardless of how aggressively the rest of the system is shaping that project. Packages are the connective tissue that makes SA's standards importable.

The hybrid policy: some things are best served by a shared package; others are cheaper to generate into the app itself. Our recommendation:

Shared
@sa/auth, service SDKs (@sa/data-warehouse-sdk, etc.), @sa/ui, design tokens, observability. Anything with a security-update obligation or a cross-app consistency requirement.
Generated
App-specific presentational pieces. The template scaffolds them into the project with SA's tokens already applied — versioning a shared package isn't worth it for a one-off card layout.
Optionally shared
Higher-level composites. When a third project wants the same composite, that triggers a short extraction conversation. Prevents drift without pre-building libraries no one ends up using.

Distribution. We recommend a private npm registry scoped to the SA org — cleanest consumption story for both Claude Code and Lovable, standard semver, no submodule or path-import gymnastics. Alternatives (GitHub Packages, JSR, git submodules) are evaluated in Appendix C — Infrastructure options.

Lovable consumption is proven. In the prototype, Lovable correctly resolves and uses @sa/ui from a workspace package today. The same mechanism extends to @sa/auth and the service SDKs.

The Lovable–engineering bridge

Business will keep using Lovable. Some of Product will too. The system has to make Lovable's output land closer to where engineering wants it — without depending on anyone changing tools.

We can definitely improve Lovable's output for engineering. The question is how far up the ladder we can climb before the maintenance cost outweighs the value. Most of what we want to do is technically achievable — the prototype demonstrates that. The remaining question, especially at the higher tiers, is per-project economics, not feasibility.

The technical mechanism that makes Tier 2+ possible. The prototype's packages/api-sdk/ holds runtime-agnostic orchestration. One piece of business logic, two thin runtime wrappers. This is what prevents the divergence trap: API behavior doesn't drift between runtimes because there is only one implementation of the behavior.

Tools across the system

Different teams use different tools. The system has to work that way.

  • Business stays in Lovable. The Lovable bridge (§2.3 + §2.5) is what makes their output usable downstream.
  • Product stays in Lovable for now; we expect them to trend toward Claude Code over time, with pairing sessions and training as part of the engagement. The system serves them either way.
  • AI Engineering works in Claude Code and their existing IDEs. The AI context layer (§2.8) makes the codebase productive from the first commit.
  • Cloud Engineering owns the substrate the template's infra/ consumes — VPCs, accounts, baselines, and (longer term) the shared CDK constructs library.

The point isn't to standardize on a single tool. It's that the template, the rules, the packages, and the AI context layer make every project look the same to whoever picks it up — regardless of what tool produced it.

Sandbox infra and CI/CD, baked in

One of the biggest handoff costs today is that a fresh project doesn't exist as a running thing until Cloud Engineering provisions it. The template removes that gap entirely.

What ships in the template:

  • infra/ using CDK. TypeScript-native, npm-installable shared constructs, no Terraform module-source path gymnastics. Lives alongside app code so a single PR can change both.
  • Shared sandbox database. Each project creates its own schema in a hosted Postgres rather than provisioning a fresh RDS instance. Drops the per-project infra tax to near zero.
  • CI/CD via .github/workflows. The first push to the repo deploys to a real sandbox URL automatically. No "wait for Cloud Engineering to set up the pipeline" step.

What gets deployed. A small, consistent set of AWS primitives in every project: shared Aurora (project-scoped schemas), ECS Fargate for the Node and Python APIs, CloudFront + S3 for the SPA, per-project S3 buckets for files, and Lambda + EventBridge for scheduled and async work. Same shape in every project — engineers who've operated one project can operate any other. The full per-primitive rationale, the runtime diagram, and the candid framing of today's bundled-Supabase-on-EC2 pattern (and why we'd move off it) live in Appendix D — Deployment architecture.

Two related-but-distinct decisions. Appendix C covers where the IaC code lives and which tool writes it (centralized Terraform vs. per-app vs. the hybrid CDK approach we recommend). Appendix D covers what that IaC actually deploys (the runtime topology above). Both need Cloud Engineering's voice; both are framed as the conversation we'd open with Ketan's team early in the engagement.

Scope. Sandbox is in scope and designed. Pre-prod and prod remain an open evaluation — same shape, different config — covered in Appendix D's "Sandbox vs. Prod" section.

Why this matters for both levers. A real sandbox environment existing the moment a project is created is the single biggest reduction in the cost of starting work. AI Engineering doesn't wait. Cloud Engineering isn't pulled in for routine work. Over time, this is the foundation that makes "Product creates a real project on their own" possible — but we don't have to commit to that endpoint to capture the value today.

AI context layer

Wired throughout the monorepo so any agent — Lovable, Claude Code, Cursor — has the context it needs everywhere it works. This is the connective tissue: the part that makes the codebase productive faster for whoever picks it up, regardless of team or tool.

  • CLAUDE.md files at the root and in each app/package. Each one tells the agent how that directory works — conventions, patterns, what's allowed where.
  • Agents (.claude/agents/) — ready-to-use workflows for routine tasks: scaffolding a component, adding an endpoint, writing a migration.
  • Skills (.claude/skills/) — documented patterns the agent follows when a task matches: adding a page, wiring up a DB table, registering a new module.
  • Lovable's parallel surface (.lovable/system.md + .lovable/rules/) — the design-system rules generated from the same source, kept in sync by CI. One source of truth, two consumers.

The result: anyone working in this codebase — SA's team, ours, a future hire, an agent on any of their behalf — is meaningfully more productive than they'd be in an equivalent codebase without this layer.

What we'd evaluate during the engagement

A real consulting engagement gives us time to test rather than guess. The items below are deliberately open — committed to as evaluations, not as decisions we've made and are quietly selling as done.

  • Stack picks. Fastify vs. NestJS for the Node API; Vite vs. Next.js for the web app. Initial leans documented in §2.2.
  • Sustained Lovable rule adherence. Initial testing is promising. The real question is how well rules hold under sustained product-team workload, multi-week iteration, and the full range of feature shapes SA builds.
  • Tier 3 economics. What dual-runtime maintenance actually costs once a real project is in active iteration. Measured, not estimated.
  • Pre-prod and prod infrastructure. Sandbox is in scope and designed; pre-prod and prod are explicitly an open conversation with Cloud Engineering. See Appendix C for the framing.
  • The shared-package roster. Which packages we build first (after @sa/auth, @sa/ui, and the most-used service SDKs), the versioning discipline, the cross-app composite-extraction policy.
  • Handoff automation. How much of the Tier 2 clean handoff can be automated as a Claude Code skill or subagent vs. handled as a documented manual flow.
